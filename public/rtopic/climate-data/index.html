Below are links to some preformatted data set related to the presentations you gave last Friday about our changing climate. I have taken the raw data files and made them neat for plotting. If you feel confident with the plotting you should try and work up the data from the raw version.

You should find your assigned data and then work to read it in and experiment with ways you might be able to plot it that would make sense. Think about what we’ve covered so far in terms of creating geoms, formatting plots, and playing around with aesthetic presentation.

Be sure to check out all relevant metadata from the linked sites to ensure you fully understand the data you are working with.

Note on wrangling

If you feel like you would enjoy the challenge of working up the data from the raw data provided on the websites of any of the data sets, you should refer to the Wrangle Chapter or R for Data Science, especially the Data Import and Tidy data chapters. There’s a whole slew of tools you will need to massage the data into a nice format, but some of the key things will be:

  • Using the read_csv(), read_tsv(), or read_delim() functions and looking at the additional arguments that can be passed to these
  • Potentially forcing a column to a numeric using as.numeric()
  • Using mutate() to create new columns in your data frame (potentially from existing data in the frame)
  • The lubridate package creates data-time formats from a variety of inputs (see Lubridate chapter in R for Data Science)
  • gather() function can take data spread over multiple columns and bring it into one place. This is especially useful if your data is organized with year on the row names and months in the columns.
  • readxl package has a read_excel() function for reading in Excel datasheets

Surface Temperature

Data is temperature anomalies compiled by NASA and available on their website. Also some good FAQs about how this is calculated. These anomalies are calculated relative to the baseline period 1951 – 1980.

  • Air surface temperature anomaly CSV
  • Air surface temp raw RAW

Fields

  • time: Time in decimal years
  • year: Integer year
  • month: Month of year
  • temp: Temperature anomaly in degrees C compared to average of 1951 – 1980.

Questions to Ponder

What do we understand by “temperature anomaly”?

Can you join this record up with the paleo record?

How best to indicate the baseline period?

Paleo Records

Data is from Antarctic ice cores and can be accessed via CDIAC which will take you to the NOAA Ice Core data site. CO2 data recovered from this (data page)[https://www.ncdc.noaa.gov/paleo-search/study/6091]. Data is a combination of records from a few cores - raw data file and site have all relevant metadata. Temperature data found here. Temperature value is relative to the average over the past 1000 years.

  • CO2 Paleo Record CSV
  • Temp Paleo Record CSV
  • CO2 Paleo Record RAW
  • Temp Paleo Record RAW

Fields

CO2 - time: Time before 2008 in years - co2: Carbon dioxide concentration in parts per million

Temp - time: Time before year 1950 - deut: Deuterum Data - temp: Temperature estimate in degrees C difference from average of past 1000 years

Questions to ponder

Can you combine the records and show how they co-vary?

Can you add them to the modern record?

Upper Ocean Temperature

Data is from NOAA. It is the yearly average of upper 100 m ocean temperature for the various ocean basins and globally.

  • Upper-ocean temperatures CSV
  • Upper-ocean temperatures RAW

Fields

  • time: Time in years
  • WO: Temperature anomaly in degrees C for the world’s oceans
  • WOse: Standard error in the Temperature anomaly
  • NH, NHse, SH, SHse: Temperature anomalies and standard errors for Northern and Southern hemispheres.

Questions to ponder

How do the NH and SH compare?

Can you download and bring in data from other depths?

Ocean Acidification

pH and carbonate chem data can be accessed from either the BATS data site or the HOTS data site for data in the Atlantic and Pacific respectively. A good raw data version of the surface carbonate chemistry in the Pacific can be found here

  • Surface Carb Chem HOTS CSV
  • Surface Carb Chem HOTS RAW

Fields

  • dttm: Date and time of record
  • DIC: Dissolved inorganic carbon in micromoles per kilogram
  • TA: Total Alkalinity in micromoles per kilogram
  • pHcalc_insitu: In situ pH
  • CO2calc_insitu: Partial pressure of CO2 in the ocean in ppm

Ice Mass Balance

Data is from IMBIE, which has annual mass loss from both Greenland and Antarctica to 2012 in one combined excel workbook. They also have a more updated Antarctic estimate and, coming soon, an updated Greenland one. Here, I include the combined data set.

  • Icesheet mass balance Greenland CSV
  • Icesheet mass balance Antarctica CSV
  • Icesheet mass balance RAW

Fields

  • time: Decimal year
  • ice_change: Change in ice mass in gigatonnes
  • ice_change_err: uncertainty in change
  • sea_level_change: contribution to sea level in mm
  • sea_level_change_err: uncertainty in the sea level contribution

Questions to ponder

How do the icesheets compare?

How significant are the losses compared to the total mass?

How much are they contributing to current sea level rise?

Sea Level Rise

Data is from CSIRO and includes both modern altimeter data and past reconstructions of sea level rise.

  • Sea Level Rise CSV
  • Sea level Rise (recon) RAW
  • Sea level Rise (alti) RAW

Fields

  • time: Decimal years
  • sea_level_alt: Altimeter sea level in mm relative to 0 mm at 1990.
  • sea_level_rec: Reconstructed sea level in mm relative to 0 mm at 1990.
  • sea_level_rec_err: 1 std err in reconstructed sea level

Questions

How were these two datasets compiled?

How well do they agree?

What is having the major impact on sea levels?
